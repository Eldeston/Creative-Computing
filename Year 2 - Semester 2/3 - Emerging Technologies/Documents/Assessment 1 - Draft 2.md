# Introduction

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a rapidly evolving technology. In this day and age, it is globally known as a powerful and practical tool for everyone in the 21st century. What started as an experimental algorithm evolving to a powerful tool changing many aspects of our lives. Massive corporate investments, widespread adoption across industries, and cultural fascination surrounding generative tools accelerated its trend. Global AI research and investments have surged dramatically since 2020, reflecting both its promise and pressure surrounding AI development according to Our World in Data by Charlie Giattino, Edouard Mathieu, Veronika Samborska, and Max Roser. The rapid growth of AI also exposed many ethical concerns that many of us are still trying to understand.

Before we continue this debate, we need to first understand what is Artificial Intelligence. Artificial Intelligence is an idea, or system that can perform tasks that a human would normally do. This ranges from learning something, communicating ideas, writing paragraphs, and solving complex problems. Its common applications are used in customer services, patient diagnosis, grammar checking, and image generation. All of which will be discussed in this debate.

We will find out in this debate on how **artificial intelligence threatens to take over too many aspects of our lives that should be left to humans**, and how we as humans can be informed and be responsible for it and my personal opinions. The debate is not about rejecting or embracing AI. It is about understanding where do we need to set its limits. The following sections explore both sides of this debate. The affirmative side argues that AI threatens essential human domains—our planet, our skills, our relationships, and our autonomy—while the negative side contends that AI, when used responsibly, can complement human abilities and improve quality of life.

# Arguments for the Affirmative

## AI’s Influence on Human Skills, Critical Thinking, and Emotional Well‑Being

One major concern is the impact of AI on human cognition and emotional health. Students and workers who rely heavily on AI tools show decreases in independent reasoning and problem‑solving abilities reported by a group of researchers on a 2025 Microsoft Research survey. The report shows young adults started using ChatGPT to help with trivial work and showing that over‑dependence on AI may erode cognitive skills and self-confidence that education systems are designed to cultivate.

This phenomenon is described as the “AI paradox,” a term used by JustThink.ai, which argues that as AI becomes more capable, humans risk becoming less capable. When AI writes essays, solves math problems, generates ideas, and even makes decisions, people may lose the ability to perform these tasks themselves. This concern is echoed in psychological research published in Psychology Today, which notes that AI tools can reduce mental effort and diminish the value of human learning.

The decline of human skills is not limited to academics. A Medium essay by Alex Northwood argues that AI is eroding human connection by replacing genuine interpersonal interactions with algorithmic simulations. When people turn to AI for emotional support, companionship, or decision‑making, they may lose the ability to navigate complex social situations or maintain meaningful relationships.

## AI’s Threat to Creativity, Security, and Personal Autonomy

AI’s ability to generate creative content has sparked intense debate about originality and ownership. A Stanford JSK article titled “Theft Is Not Fair Use” argues that many AI models are trained on copyrighted material without permission, effectively appropriating the labor of artists, writers, and musicians. This concern gained public attention when Polygon reported that a video game was disqualified from the 2025 Game Awards due to suspected AI‑generated assets, raising questions about authenticity and fairness in creative industries.

Generative AI can produce impressive creative outputs, but it also warns that AI lacks the lived experience, cultural context, and emotional depth that define human creativity according to an article in Harvard Online. Adobe, a major creative software company, re-rolled its Terms Of Use after massive backlash from users fearing their content will be used by company to train its investments in Generative AI in 2024. Adobe pledged to never use user content on Generative AI according to Kate Irwin on PCMag.

AI systems can be exploited to generate sophisticated cyberattacks, automate phishing, and bypass traditional security measures. Science News Today warns that AI-driven cyber threats are becoming more advanced, making it harder for individuals and organizations to protect their data. ISSIVS reports that AI-powered surveillance tools can track individuals with unprecedented precision, raising ethical concerns about privacy and civil liberties. IBM’s analysis of AI privacy issues highlights the risks of data misuse, algorithmic bias, and unauthorized access to personal information.

## AI and the Workforce: Misjudgment, Redundancy, and Corporate Overreach

Another significant concern is the impact of AI on employment. Researchers at MIT have found that 95% of Gen AI pilot projects fail, and only a few have significant business value. Still, companies are laying off employees based on the potential of AI rather than actual performance. This has been emphasized in the article published in Harvard Business Review in 2026.

Companies like Klarna have also become a clear example of the potential risks of adopting AI. Klarna adopted AI to replace thousands of their employees but later shifted their focus to human resources due to declining service quality and customer dissatisfaction, as emphasized in the article published in Mind the Product in 2025. Information Age has also emphasized the fact that companies have shifted their focus to human resources due to the realization that AI has failed to provide the expected results. An international study conducted by Orgvue in 2025 found that 55% of businesses admitted to having made poor decisions in replacing human employees with AI. The potential adoption of AI may be due to the hype around AI rather than actual performance.

# Arguments for the Negative

## AI as a Tool for Human Progress and Innovation

While the risks risks of AI are acknowledged AI can be used responsibly to enhance human life. IBM’s overview of AI emphasizes that AI is fundamentally a tool—one that can improve efficiency, support decision‑making, and expand human capabilities when used ethically. University of Cincinnati Online highlights AI’s benefits across industries, including healthcare, education, and business, where AI can automate repetitive tasks and free humans to focus on more meaningful work.

SCS Singapore similarly notes that generative AI can accelerate innovation, support creativity, and improve productivity when integrated thoughtfully. MIT Sloan research shows that humans and AI work best together when each performs tasks suited to their strengths—AI handling data‑heavy analysis and humans providing judgment, empathy, and ethical reasoning.

## AI’s Potential to Improve Work and Reduce Burden

IBM’s insights on the future of work argue that AI can create new job opportunities, support workers, and reduce burnout by automating tedious tasks. Harvard Business Review reports that some companies lay off workers not because AI performs better, but because they anticipate future AI potential—suggesting that the problem lies in corporate decision‑making, not AI itself.

Thomson Reuters adds that AI can support talent development when used responsibly, helping employees learn new skills and adapt to changing industries.

## AI as a Tool for Environmental Solutions

IBM’s findings on the future of work suggest that AI may provide more job opportunities, aid employees, and prevent burnout by performing mundane tasks. Harvard Business Review states that some firms are firing employees not because AI performs better, but owing to the future potential of AI. This implies that the issue lies within the minds of firms, not AI technology.

Thomson Reuters states that AI may aid in talent development if well managed, helping employees learn new things and cope with a changing industry. In this regard, AI technology is not a threat to human work but a catalyst for change.

# Conclusion

Artificial intelligence currently impacts aspects of daily life. Research, investigative reports, and reviews by experts in this debate have pointed out four main risks associated with artificial intelligence. These are erosion of critical thinking, weakening personal relationships, pressure on originality, and increased security risks. There are also benefits to artificial intelligence. These are new products, smoother operations, and overall progress. This, however, depends on the careful management and operation of the technology.

Artificial intelligence does not have a set morality, it is like a double-edged sword. It operates by the objectives and values set by those who create and use it. The challenge, however, lies in creating laws that keep human decisions at the forefront. This includes establishing clear ethical measures, transparent processes, and a hierarchy for human welfare to guide the technology in expanding, and not limiting, human characteristics.
